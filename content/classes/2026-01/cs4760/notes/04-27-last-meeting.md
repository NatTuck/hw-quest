## How to Keep Up with Technology

This is something we all need to do.

Contrary to popular belief, the core stuff that these degrees cover doesn't go
bad. Even the modern math is hundred years old. The CS theory is mostly 50 years
old. The core of Robotics and IT haven't changed that much over the past 20
years.

But the details change. Constantly. And the new details mostly don't replace the
old details, they just pile up.

So an important ongoing task we have as technologists is to keep up with those
details. New tools. New programming languages. New techniques.

When I first used computers as a kid, they were home computers with no
connection to the internet. At that point, call it 1990, some businesses had
already widely adopted email.

Let's look at our disciplines over time since then. I could start earlier - all
of our disciplines had existed for a while at that point - but it'd just be more
of the same.

**1995 — The early web**
- Netscape Navigator IPO kicks off the commercial internet; "web pages" become a household concept.
- Amazon (1994) and eBay (1995) launch, proving the web can be a marketplace.
- JavaScript debuts in Netscape 2.0, making pages interactive for the first time.
- The "information superhighway" enters mainstream political and media discourse.

**2000 — The dot-com boom**
- The NASDAQ peaks in March; Pets.com and countless others flame out by year's end.
- Broadband (cable/DSL) begins displacing dial-up, making the web actually usable.
- The "build it and they will come" business model dies; unit economics briefly become fashionable again.
- Google AdWords (2000) quietly establishes a revenue model that actually works.

**2005 — The modern web, the early smartphone**
- AJAX goes mainstream (Google Maps, Gmail), turning web pages into applications without reloads.
- YouTube (2005), Reddit (2005), and Facebook (expanding beyond colleges) usher in user-generated content and social graphs.
- The Motorola Q, Palm Treo, and BlackBerry dominate; "smartphone" means a phone with email.
- Amazon Web Services launches (2006, but the S3/EC2 beta era starts the cloud conversation).

**2010 — Attack of the cloud, and the modern smartphone**
- The iPhone 4 and Samsung Galaxy S define the modern touchscreen smartphone; Android passes BlackBerry in market share.
- AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud make "renting computers" standard; startups stop buying servers.
- The iPad launches, creating the tablet category and killing the netbook within two years.
- Instagram (2010) and WhatsApp signal that mobile-native apps, not desktop ports, are the future.

**2015 — Victory of the browser**
- Chrome becomes the world's dominant browser, surpassing 50% market share; Internet Explorer enters hospice.
- Progressive Web Apps and WebAssembly blur the line between browser and native app.
- Slack, Notion, Figma, and Google Docs prove that serious work happens entirely in the browser.
- HTML5 finally kills Flash, making the browser the universal runtime.

**2020 — Victory of the smartphone**
- Global mobile traffic firmly exceeds desktop; in many countries, the smartphone is the primary (or only) computer.
- TikTok becomes the defining app, cementing short-form vertical video as the dominant content format.
- 5G rollout begins, though its impact remains more theoretical than realized.
- App Store and Play Store economies mature; mobile gaming revenue surpasses console and PC combined.

**2025 — Machine learning ruins everything**
- Large language models and generative AI become default features in search, office suites, IDEs, and creative tools.
- "AI slop" floods content platforms; trust in text, image, and video authenticity collapses.
- NVIDIA becomes one of the world's most valuable companies; the AI chip shortage reshapes geopolitics.
- Regulatory panic (EU AI Act, US executive orders) and copyright lawsuits struggle to catch up with deployment speed.
